Side quest · お遍路

Shikoku 88 — pilgrim's notes

The 1,200 km Buddhist pilgrimage circumnavigating Shikoku island, visiting all 88 temples associated with Kūkai (Kōbō Daishi). What I need to know to plan it properly, walking 20–30 km a day.

At a glance: ~1,200 km · 88 temples · 4 prefectures · ~48–55 walking days at 25 km/day average + ~6–8 rest days ≈ 8 weeks total. Start at Temple 1 (Ryōzen-ji, Tokushima), finish at Temple 88 (Ōkubo-ji, Kagawa). Traditional bonus: return to T1 and continue to Mount Kōya (Wakayama) to "close" the loop.

The realistic plan for 2027 — a 3-week sakura trip (Kumano Kodo + Shikoku T1–T23 entirely on foot, earning the Dual Pilgrim certificate) and a ~3.5-week autumn trip completing T24–T88 via selective walking + public transport for full kechigan — is in the next section.

2027 plan — sakura then autumn

Two trips, each capped at three weeks. Spring is Kumano Kodo plus the first prefecture of Shikoku (T1–T23) in cherry-blossom. Autumn is Kōchi (T24–T39) in kōyō autumn-leaf season. Shikoku explicitly supports completing the 88 in chunks — kugiri-uchi — so the nōkyōchō stays open for future years.

The certificate hook: the spring trip earns the Dual Pilgrim certificate at Kumano Hongū Taisha — the only reciprocal pilgrim recognition in the world. It requires a finished Camino de Santiago (already in the bag) plus a qualifying Kumano Kodo walk. Apply at Hongū, show the compostela, receive a printed certificate and a small enamel pin. No equivalent in any other walking culture.

Autumn 2027 — late October to mid-November (kōyō)

~3.5 weeks · T24 → T88 — walking + public transport hybrid

After spring's all-on-foot first prefecture, autumn shifts to a selective walking approach: skip the long empty stretches by local bus or train, walk the dense temple clusters where the path actually adds something. Take public transport directly to T24 at Cape Muroto to start — no walk-in from T23. Total walking ~266 km (including a Yokomine-ji day-hike with light pack); total transport ~570 km. Reaches Ōkubo-ji (T88) in ~27 days door-to-door (with a Kotohira / Kompira-san rest day in Kagawa, and a deliberate split of the final bus-heavy and walking-heavy Kagawa days for resilience).

What this autumn earns: kechigan — completing all 88 — at Ōkubo-ji. Receive the special kechigan stamp there. Mail the completed nōkyōchō to the Shikoku 88 Association afterward for the formal paper certificate (~¥3,000). Combined with the spring Dual Pilgrim, that's both certificates in one calendar year. Mount Kōya remains as the optional closing pilgrimage in a future spring.

Day 17 — the smart Yokomine-ji approach

  • Base for two nights at lodging in Saijō (T59 side) or near Kōfuku-ji T61 — either works.
  • Leave the main pack at the lodging. Take a bus or local taxi to a Yokomine trailhead, stripping ~15 km of road approach.
  • Hike the steep final stretch to T60 on a light daypack — get the stamp, photos, descent via the same route or an alternate trail.
  • Bus/taxi back to base. Sleep in the same place.
  • Day 18 starts fresh for the gentle T61 → T64 walk.

Why Kagawa is split across Days 24 + 25

The most fragile element of the Kagawa stretch is the T82 → T83 → T84 transit chain — Kotoden Kotohira Line from Takamatsu west to Ichinomiya, back through central Takamatsu, then Kotoden Shido Line + Yashima shuttle bus up the plateau. Two bus legs that have to land before stamp offices close at 17:00. Pairing that with a 20 km walk T84 → T87 in the same day means one slipped bus cascades into a missed stamp.

The 27-day plan separates the risk: Day 24 is bus-only (stamps T83 and T84, no walking deadline pressure), Day 25 is walking-only (T84 → T87, no transit deadlines). If a bus is late on Day 24 you just shift the rest of the day later. If the legs are slow on Day 25 you just slow down.

Tighter alternative (saves 1 day): merge into a single Day 24 — start at 7am, T82 area → T83 by 9am, T84 by noon, walk T84 → T87 with stamp office checks. Only do this if buses and weather are cooperating; otherwise the split below is the safer call.

Notes on the transport segments

  • Most transit hops are local JR trains + community buses. The Shikoku 88 Association map shows which buses run between which temples; the Henro Service app does too.
  • The Ashizuri detour (T37 → T38 → T39) takes a full day by bus — Cape Ashizuri is a long peninsula out and back.
  • Day 10 is stamp-office sensitive. Set out 7am from Sukumo. T39 Enkō-ji first, then bus to T40 Kanjizai-ji (Uwajima), then continue to T41 area before evening. Have a taxi number ready if Sukumo–Uwajima buses are sparse.
  • T65 → T66 involves Unpen-ji at altitude. The Unpen-ji ropeway saves a 700 m climb.
  • Day 24 (bus-only) + Day 25 (walking-only) — the deliberate Kagawa split. Day 24 just needs to land T83 and T84 stamps; Day 25 walks T84 → T87 unhurried. Reduces the cascade risk of a single missed bus.

How this plan handles the three goals

  • 3-week ceiling — softened. Spring holds at 3 weeks. Autumn now ~3.9 weeks (27 days), prioritising resilience over speed: three rest days (Kōchi City Day 6, Dōgo Onsen Day 15, Kotohira Day 21) and the Kagawa finish is split into a bus-only stamp day (24) and a walking-only day (25) so a missed bus doesn't cost a stamp. Squeeze options: merge Day 24 + 25 back into one day and skip a rest day → 25.
  • Certificates — both, same year. Spring earns the Dual Pilgrim at Hongū (Camino + Kumano). Autumn earns the kechigan completion at Ōkubo-ji. Two of the most uncommon pilgrim recognitions, twelve months apart.
  • Walk-vs-bus balance — exactly the user's brief. Spring T1–T23 is entirely on foot (the slow, social, formative half). Autumn is selectively walked — ~255 km on foot, ~596 km by transit, every temple visited.
  • Social — front-loaded in spring. Kumano Kodo Nakahechi is the most internationally walked path in Japan — shared dinner tables every night. Spring Tokushima is the most-walked Shikoku stretch. Autumn is quieter, but the Kagawa finish week brings the pilgrim density back as everyone converges on Ōkubo-ji.

Kumano Kodo — practicalities

  • Dual Pilgrim credential: pick up the small fold-out booklet at the Tanabe City Kumano Tourism Bureau before starting (free). Get stamps along the way at oji shrines and at each Grand Shrine. Apply at Hongū Taisha with your Camino compostela — ~¥1,000, takes 10 minutes.
  • Lodging: book months ahead for sakura season. kumano-travel.com is the English-language booking hub run by the tourism bureau.
  • Yunomine Onsen: tiny village 90 minutes' walk past Hongū. Tsubo-yu is the only UNESCO-listed hot-spring bath in the world — a single thatched cabin on the river, ~30 minutes per person, ticket from the village office. Worth the detour.
  • The boat down the Kumano River from Hitari to Shingu was historically how pilgrims travelled between Hongū and Hayatama. A traditional kawabune still runs the route on a schedule. Book in advance.
  • Nachi Taisha + Nachi-no-Taki: the 133 m waterfall is the largest in Japan and the actual object of pilgrimage at Nachi — the shrine grew up around it. Stay overnight at Nachi-Katsuura for tuna markets at 6am the next morning.

Kugiri-uchi — completing Shikoku across multiple years

Most modern walking pilgrims who can't take 8 weeks off do exactly this. Your nōkyōchō survives between trips. Stamps don't expire. You don't need to walk consecutively. The Association recognises completion whenever you reach all 88 — could be next year, could be a decade from now. Once finished, you mail the book in for the certificate.

For this plan, the whole pilgrimage fits inside 2027 itself:

  • 2027 Spring: Kumano Kodo + Shikoku T1–T23 — Dual Pilgrim certificate at Hongū Taisha
  • 2027 Autumn: Shikoku T24–T88 — kechigan at Ōkubo-ji
  • Any later spring: Mount Kōya — ohenro-modori at Okunoin where Kūkai is interred. The traditional close. Long weekend.

Note: kugiri-uchi is still useful if either trip needs to shorten mid-way — your nōkyōchō survives between any trips, so you can always pick up where you left off.

Getting to the start — Temple 1 (Ryōzen-ji, Tokushima)

Ryōzen-ji is in Naruto City, Tokushima Prefecture. Closest station: JR Bandō, a 10-minute walk to the temple.

From Tokyo

  • Train (4–5 hrs): Tōkaidō/Sanyō Shinkansen Tokyo → Okayama (~3:20), then Marine Liner to Takamatsu (~1 hr), then JR Kōtoku Line to Bandō (~1 hr). Total ~5–6 hrs incl. transfers.
  • Plane (3 hrs door-to-door): Haneda → Tokushima Airport (~75 min flight), then limousine bus to Tokushima Station (~30 min), then train to Bandō (~30 min).

From Osaka / Kansai Airport

  • Bus (~3 hrs): Osaka Umeda → Tokushima Highway Bus. From Tokushima Station, JR to Bandō.
  • Train: Shin-Osaka → Okayama (~50 min Shinkansen) → Takamatsu (Marine Liner) → Bandō.

Tip

Most pilgrims spend the first night in Tokushima City the day before and start fresh at Ryōzen-ji in the morning. Buy your hakui, sugegasa, nōkyōchō, and walking stick at the temple's henro shop right there — it's the traditional first act.

Getting back from the end — Temple 88 (Ōkubo-ji, Kagawa)

Ōkubo-ji is in Sanuki City, Kagawa, on a hillside above Nagao town.

  • From Ōkubo-ji: walk or taxi to JR Nagao Station (~7 km).
  • JR Nagao → Takamatsu (~50 min). Takamatsu is the main hub.
  • From Takamatsu:
    • Marine Liner → Okayama (~1 hr) → Shinkansen to Tokyo (~3:30) or Osaka (~45 min).
    • Fly: Takamatsu Airport → Tokyo Haneda (~80 min).

Optional: continue to Mount Kōya

Traditional closing: after the 88, take the train to Wakayama, then Nankai Railway to Gokurakubashi, then cable car up to Mount Kōya. Stay at one of the temple shukubō (Eko-in is popular), pay respects at Okunoin where Kūkai is interred. Add 2–3 days.

Stamps — the nōkyōchō

The book that is the pilgrimage.

  1. Buy a nōkyōchō at Temple 1. ~¥2,500–3,500 depending on binding. (Or a hanging scroll / robe-stamp version for the obsessive.)
  2. At each temple: ring the bell, recite the heart sutra (or just stand quietly), then go to the nōkyō-sho (stamp office) and hand over the book. The monk writes the temple name and main deity in calligraphy and adds three vermilion seals.
  3. Cost: ¥300 per stamp. So ¥26,400 total for all 88. Cash only.
  4. Hours: 7:00 – 17:00 typically. Off-season some close at 16:00. Plan your daily endpoint to arrive before close.
  5. Etiquette: hat off, bell rung, sutras left in the offering box if you carry them. Don't take the book apart. Don't take photos in the stamp office.

Completion — the Shikoku version of the Compostela

Shikoku doesn't issue a Compostela like Santiago. The nōkyōchō with 88 stamps is itself the certificate. But there are layers of "completion":

  • Kechigan (結願) — finishing the 88th temple. Receive a special kechigan stamp at Ōkubo-ji.
  • Ohenro-modori (お遍路戻り) — returning to Temple 1 to "close the circle." Many do this; some walk back the ~40 km from Ōkubo-ji to Ryōzen-ji over 1–2 days.
  • Mount Kōya pilgrimage — pay respects at Okunoin where Kūkai is interred. The traditional final step. Receive the stamp at Okunoin and your book is "closed."
  • Official completion certificate — issued by the Shikoku 88-temple Pilgrimage Association (四国八十八ヶ所霊場会). Submit your completed nōkyōchō by mail. ~¥3,000. They send a paper certificate in return.

Where to stay — the lodging menu

  • Shukubō (宿坊) — temple lodging. ~30–40 temples on the route offer this. ¥6,000–8,000 with dinner + breakfast. Vegetarian shōjin-ryōri, morning sutras. Book ahead. Top picks: Anraku-ji (T6), Iwamoto-ji (T37), Zentsū-ji (T75).
  • Minshuku (民宿) — family-run inns. ¥6,000–9,000 with two meals. Traditional, often very pilgrim-friendly. The default for most nights.
  • Ryokan (旅館) — fancier traditional inns. ¥10,000–18,000. Save for rest-day towns.
  • Business hotels — ¥5,000–8,000. Wifi, laundry. Best for rest days and stops in bigger towns (Tokushima, Kōchi, Matsuyama, Takamatsu).
  • Henro-goya (遍路小屋) — ~80 free wooden shelters built by supporters. Roof and a bench, sometimes a toilet. Sleeping in them is fine for emergencies; not a daily plan.
  • Tsuyado / zenkonyado (通夜堂 / 善根宿) — free pilgrim lodging, usually at temples or homes. Very basic. A small gratuity (¥500–1,000) is good form. Ask in town if anyone offers "tsuyadō wa arimasuka?"

Gear — the pilgrim kit

  • Hakui (白衣) — white robe. Bought at T1. Don't replace it when it gets dirty; it earns the wear.
  • Sugegasa (菅笠) — conical hat. Bought at T1. Rain + sun. The inscription reads "Dōgyō-ninin" — "we walk together (with Kōbō Daishi)."
  • Kongō-zue (金剛杖) — walking stick. Bought at T1. Symbolically is Kōbō Daishi walking with you. Wash its tip before entering lodging. Don't tap it on bridges (out of respect — Kōbō Daishi is said to be sleeping there).
  • Nōkyōchō, juzu (beads), bell, sutra book — the rest of the kit.
  • Practical: good trail shoes (broken in), one set of warm clothes (mountains get cold even in summer), rain gear, blister tape, sunscreen, 20 L pack max. Lodgings do laundry — pack light.

Budget

For a 55-day walking pilgrimage, planning to stay in minshuku/shukubō most nights:

  • Lodging (~50 nights paid): ¥350,000 (~€2,200)
  • Stamps: ¥26,400
  • Lunch + snacks on the trail: ¥80,000 (~€500)
  • Trains + buses (transit days): ¥30,000
  • Gear at T1: ¥15,000–25,000
  • Total: roughly ¥500,000 (~€3,200) for the trip body, before international flights and Mount Kōya add-on.

Japan is still cash-heavy outside cities — pull a big chunk at a Post Office or 7-Eleven ATM in Tokushima before starting.

When to go

  • Best: March – early May, and October – November. Cherry blossom or autumn colour, mild temperatures.
  • Avoid: June–July (rainy season) and August (oppressively humid). January–February is doable but cold in mountain passes.
  • Many do it in spring; expect more shared shukubō rooms and friendlier "fellow ohenro" energy.

Things they don't tell you

  • Locals call walking pilgrims ohenro-san and routinely offer osettai — gifts of food, drink, sometimes money. You must accept (refusing is rude). Carry small osamefuda cards to give back as thanks.
  • Most pilgrims today do the route by bus or car. Walking pilgrims are rare and respected. You'll be treated very kindly.
  • The hardest stretch is days 24–28 (Kōchi west coast). Long, empty, beautiful, a bit demoralising. Plan a real rest day after.
  • "Henrokorogashi" (遍路ころがし) = "pilgrim killers." Days T11→T12, T20→T21, the Yokomine-ji climb, and the T87→T88 finish. Steep, sometimes muddy, often wonderful.
  • If a day's plan falls apart, there's almost always a JR or local bus you can use to skip a stretch. The pilgrimage doesn't mind. "Walk what you can, accept what you can't."
